# CSM Best Practices Playbook

A collection of frameworks, scripts, and strategies that actually work in SaaS Customer Success. Not theory — practical stuff you can use tomorrow.

---

## Discovery & Onboarding

### The First Call Framework

Your first real conversation sets the tone for the entire relationship. Here's what to cover — and more importantly, what to listen for.

**Before the call:**
- Review the sales handoff notes (and take them with a grain of salt — the customer's version of why they bought is often different from the AE's)
- Check their product usage data if any exists from a trial
- Look up the company on LinkedIn — understand their business, not just their use case
- Prepare 3-4 open-ended questions specific to their situation

**On the call:**

Open with context, not a pitch. Something like:
> "I've reviewed what [AE name] shared about your goals, but I'd love to hear it directly from you — what does success look like for your team in the first 90 days?"

Key discovery questions:
1. "What problem were you solving when you started looking for a tool like ours?"
2. "Who else on your team will be using this, and what does their day-to-day look like?"
3. "Have you used something similar before? What worked and what didn't?"
4. "What would make you say 'this was absolutely worth it' in six months?"
5. "Is there anything about the buying process or onboarding that's made you nervous?"

That last question is gold. It surfaces concerns early when you can still address them.

**After the call:**
- Send a recap email within 2 hours (not the next day)
- Log the call in your tracker with key insights
- Create the first set of action items
- Set up the onboarding project in your Projects database

### 30/60/90 Day Onboarding Cadence

**Days 1-30: Foundation**
- Week 1: Kickoff call, technical setup, admin training
- Week 2: Core feature walkthrough for primary users
- Week 3: First milestone check-in — are they using the basics?
- Week 4: Broader team rollout begins, address friction points

Success signal: Primary users are logging in at least 3x per week.

**Days 31-60: Adoption**
- Bi-weekly check-ins focused on usage patterns
- Advanced feature introduction (only when basics are solid)
- Connect them with other customers via community or case studies
- Identify internal champions and support them

Success signal: Multiple users engaged, initial use case fully operational.

**Days 61-90: Value Realization**
- First value review — can they articulate ROI?
- Introduce expansion use cases (gently)
- QBR planning begins
- Gather first NPS/CSAT feedback

Success signal: Customer can explain the value to someone who wasn't in the buying process.

## Account Health Management

### Health Score Framework

Don't just gut-feel your health scores. Use a weighted model.

| Factor | Weight | Green | Yellow | Red |
|--------|--------|-------|--------|-----|
| Product Usage | 30% | Active daily, multiple users | Declining or single user | Minimal/no login in 14+ days |
| Engagement | 25% | Responds promptly, proactive | Slow responses, reactive only | Ghosting, canceled meetings |
| Support | 15% | Few tickets, positive CSAT | Increasing tickets, neutral | Escalations, negative CSAT |
| Sentiment | 15% | Enthusiastic, refers others | Neutral, transactional | Frustrated, exploring alternatives |
| Business Fit | 15% | Growing, expanding use | Stable, status quo | Layoffs, budget cuts, pivot |

Red doesn't mean they're churning. It means you need a plan. Now.

### Handling At-Risk Accounts

When an account goes red, here's a working framework:

1. **Diagnose** — What specifically triggered the risk? Don't guess. Talk to the customer, check support tickets, review usage data.

2. **Escalate Internally** — Loop in your manager. Not because you can't handle it, but because two perspectives are better than one and you may need resources.

3. **Create a Recovery Plan** — Specific actions with dates. "We'll improve the relationship" is not a plan. "Schedule executive sponsor call by Friday, deliver custom training session by end of next week, resolve open support tickets within 48 hours" is a plan.

4. **Execute and Document** — Do the things. Track every interaction. This documentation protects you and helps the next person if the account transitions.

5. **Reassess** — After 30 days, has the trajectory changed? If yes, great — document what worked. If no, escalate further.

## QBR (Quarterly Business Review)

### When to QBR and When Not To

Hot take: not every account needs a QBR. A $5K ARR SMB account with one user doesn't need a 60-minute business review. They need a 15-minute check-in and a helpful email.

**QBR-worthy accounts:**
- Enterprise and mid-market tier
- Strategic accounts regardless of ARR
- Accounts approaching renewal (within 90 days)
- At-risk accounts that need a reset
- Expansion-ready accounts

### QBR Structure

Total time: 45-60 minutes. Not longer. Nobody wants a 90-minute QBR.

**1. Value Delivered (10 min)**
Lead with outcomes, not product features. Show them what they've accomplished.
- Usage stats with context (not just numbers — what do the numbers mean?)
- Goals from last QBR vs. actuals
- ROI calculations if you can quantify them

**2. What's Working and What Isn't (10 min)**
Be honest. If something isn't going well, name it first. This builds trust faster than pretending everything is great.
- Areas of strength
- Challenges and your plan to address them
- Open support items and status

**3. Roadmap & What's Next (10 min)**
Share relevant upcoming features. Don't do a full product demo — just the stuff that matters to them specifically.
- Features shipping that align with their use case
- Beta programs they could join
- Feedback you've relayed to Product on their behalf

**4. Their Business & Strategic Goals (15 min)**
This is the most important part. Shut up and listen.
- What's changing in their business?
- New initiatives that could expand their use case?
- Team changes, budget changes, priority shifts?
- What do they need from you in the next quarter?

**5. Action Items & Next Steps (5 min)**
- Recap every action item with owners and dates
- Confirm next QBR date
- Ask if there's anything else on their mind

### Post-QBR Checklist
- [ ] Send recap email within 24 hours
- [ ] Log all action items in your task tracker
- [ ] Update account health score
- [ ] Log any wins or expansion signals in Brag Book
- [ ] Share relevant feedback with Product team
- [ ] Calendar the next QBR

## Expansion & Upsell

### Recognizing Expansion Signals

These aren't subtle. Train yourself to listen for them:

- "We've been thinking about rolling this out to [other team]"
- "Is there a way to do [thing that's in a higher tier]?"
- "We're hiring a bunch of people in Q3"
- "Our other office/region has been asking about this"
- High usage approaching plan limits
- Power users building creative workarounds for missing features
- Executives joining QBRs who weren't involved before

### The Natural Expansion Conversation

Don't pitch. Connect value to need.

Bad: "I noticed you're on the Pro plan. Our Enterprise plan has some great features I think you'd love."

Better: "You mentioned wanting to roll this out to the London team. Currently your plan supports up to 50 users — if the London team is around 30 people, we should talk about what that looks like on the licensing side. Want me to put together a few options?"

The difference? One is selling. The other is solving a problem they already told you about.

## Escalation Management

### When to Escalate

Escalate when:
- The customer has asked for your manager or "someone higher up"
- A critical business process is broken and the fix isn't happening fast enough
- You've exhausted your internal resources and still can't resolve the issue
- Churn risk is real and imminent
- There's a contractual or legal dimension you can't navigate alone

Don't escalate when:
- You just want someone else to deal with a difficult conversation
- The customer is being unreasonable and you want backup to say no (though sometimes this IS valid)
- You haven't actually tried to resolve it yourself yet

### Escalation Documentation Template

When you escalate, bring the full picture:

```
ACCOUNT: [Name] | ARR: [Amount] | Renewal: [Date]
ISSUE: [One sentence summary]
TIMELINE: [When it started, what's happened so far]
IMPACT: [Business impact to the customer]
WHAT I'VE TRIED: [Actions taken and results]
WHAT I NEED: [Specific ask — engineering time, exec sponsor call, pricing flexibility, etc.]
URGENCY: [Why this can't wait]
```

Your manager will love you for this. Guaranteed.

## Churn Prevention

### The "Save" Conversation

When a customer tells you they're leaving, here's the framework:

1. **Don't panic.** Take a breath. This isn't personal.

2. **Thank them for telling you.** Seriously. "I appreciate you being direct with me" goes a long way.

3. **Understand the full picture.** Ask: "Help me understand what's driving this decision. Is there one thing or is it a combination?"

4. **Separate fixable from unfixable.** Budget cuts and company pivots are usually unfixable. Product gaps, poor experiences, and lack of value are fixable — sometimes.

5. **If it's fixable, present a plan.** Concrete. Specific. Time-bound. "If we could [specific solution] within [timeframe], would that change the conversation?"

6. **If it's not fixable, make the exit graceful.** Help them export data. Be genuinely helpful. Today's churned customer is next year's re-sign if you handle it well.

7. **Document everything.** The reason, the conversation, what you offered, the outcome. Your CS team needs this data.

### Proactive Churn Prevention Checklist

Run this monthly for your top accounts:

- [ ] Have I spoken with this account in the last 30 days?
- [ ] Do I have relationships beyond one contact? (Multi-threading)
- [ ] Is usage trending up, flat, or down?
- [ ] Are there unresolved support tickets older than 7 days?
- [ ] Has the customer achieved their stated success metrics?
- [ ] Am I aware of any business changes (layoffs, restructuring, M&A)?
- [ ] Is there a champion actively advocating internally?
- [ ] Have I delivered value beyond the product (insights, intros, content)?

## Internal Stakeholder Management

### Working with Product

Product teams are drowning in feature requests. Here's how to be the CSM they actually listen to:

- **Aggregate, don't relay.** "5 enterprise accounts representing $2M ARR have requested X" hits differently than "my customer wants X."
- **Frame it as impact.** Revenue at risk, expansion blocked, competitive pressure.
- **Provide context.** What's the customer trying to accomplish? What's the workaround they're using now?
- **Be honest about priority.** Not everything is urgent. Crying wolf burns trust.

### Working with Support

- Don't just escalate everything. Understand the issue first.
- Provide reproduction steps when possible.
- Follow up on open tickets — don't assume they're being handled.
- Give positive feedback when Support does great work. They hear complaints all day.

### Working with Sales

- Give clean, detailed handoffs when passing expansion opps to AEs.
- Share customer context they'd never get from CRM data alone.
- Be clear about what the customer has been told vs. what's actually available.
- Don't let AEs overpromise to your existing customers. Push back when needed.

## Communication Frameworks

### The STAR Method for Customer Emails

When delivering updates, structure matters:

- **S**ituation: What's the context?
- **T**ask: What was the goal or need?
- **A**ction: What did you/the team do?
- **R**esult: What's the outcome or next step?

### The "Sandwich" Is Dead

Stop doing the feedback sandwich (positive-negative-positive). Customers see through it. Just be direct and respectful.

Instead:
> "I want to flag something. [Issue]. Here's what we're doing about it: [Action]. You should see [Result] by [Date]. Does that work for your timeline?"

Straightforward. Honest. Professional.
