# Performance Review Guide

How to use this toolkit to actually crush your performance reviews — instead of scrambling to remember what you did.

---

## The Core Problem

Here's what happens to most CSMs at review time: your manager asks you to write a self-assessment, and you spend three miserable hours scrolling through Slack, email, and your calendar trying to reconstruct six months of work. Half of your best accomplishments? Forgotten. The impact numbers? Gone. That amazing save you pulled off in April? You can barely remember the account name.

This guide solves that by building a continuous documentation habit that makes review prep almost automatic.

---

## The Brag Book System

### Why It Works

Your Brag Book database isn't vanity — it's career insurance. Here's the thing about performance reviews: they're not really about what you did. They're about what you can *prove* you did, articulated in a way your manager can repeat to their manager.

### What to Log (And What People Usually Miss)

**The obvious wins:**
- Saved a churning account (include ARR preserved)
- Closed an expansion (include revenue added)
- Onboarded a new customer successfully
- Hit a retention or NPS target

**The wins people forget to document:**
- Built a process that your team adopted
- Mentored a new CSM through their first QBR
- Identified a product bug that affected multiple accounts
- Created a resource (template, guide, training) other people use
- Got a customer to be a reference, join a case study, or speak at an event
- Caught a risk signal early and prevented a fire before it started
- Provided feedback to Product that actually shipped
- Navigated a difficult cross-functional situation
- Managed through a customer crisis without escalation

### How to Write a Good Brag Book Entry

Bad entry:
> "Helped Acme Corp with their renewal."

Good entry:
> "Led renewal negotiation for Acme Corp ($180K ARR) that was at risk due to executive sponsor departure. Built relationship with new VP of Ops over 6 weeks, conducted business impact review showing 34% efficiency gain, and secured a 2-year renewal with 15% uplift. Collaborated with AE and Solutions Engineer on custom proposal."

The difference? Specificity, quantified impact, and context about the difficulty.

### The IMPACT Framework for Win Documentation

Use this structure every time:

- **I**nitiative — What did you do? What was the action?
- **M**etric — How do you measure the result?
- **P**roblem — What problem existed before your action?
- **A**ction — What specifically did you do (steps, not just outcomes)?
- **C**ollaboration — Who else was involved? (Giving credit is strategic, not just nice.)
- **T**imeframe — How long did it take? Urgency matters.

## Preparing for Your Self-Assessment

### Step 1: Pull Your Data (15 minutes)

Open your Brag Book and filter for the review period. Sort by revenue impact descending. You want your top 8-10 wins visible.

Then check:
- Your account retention rate for the period
- Total ARR managed and retained
- NRR (Net Revenue Retention) if you can calculate it
- NPS/CSAT scores for your accounts
- Number of QBRs conducted
- Expansion revenue closed or influenced

### Step 2: Pick Your Narrative Themes (15 minutes)

Don't just list wins. Group them into 3-4 themes that tell a story about who you are as a CSM.

Example themes:
- "I'm a retention specialist who thrives in complex, at-risk situations"
- "I build scalable processes that make the whole team better"
- "I drive expansion by deeply understanding customer business goals"
- "I'm the cross-functional bridge between CS, Product, and Support"

Pick 3 themes max. Support each with 2-3 specific wins from your Brag Book.

### Step 3: Quantify Everything (20 minutes)

For each win, make sure you have at least one hard number:
- Revenue: ARR saved, expanded, or influenced
- Efficiency: Time saved, process improvements quantified
- Scale: Number of accounts impacted, team members enabled
- Customer metrics: NPS increase, adoption rate improvement, time-to-value reduction

If you can't find an exact number, estimate conservatively and say so. "Approximately $50K ARR at risk" is better than "a big account was going to churn."

### Step 4: Write the Self-Assessment (30 minutes)

Structure it this way:

**Summary (2-3 sentences)**
Your overall narrative. What you accomplished, the impact, and what you're proud of.

**Theme 1: [Name]**
- Context for why this matters
- Win 1 with metrics
- Win 2 with metrics
- What you learned

**Theme 2: [Name]**
- Same structure

**Theme 3: [Name]**
- Same structure

**Growth Areas**
- Be honest about 1-2 areas for development
- Show you're already working on them ("I've started doing X to address this")
- Frame them as growth opportunities, not failures

**Goals for Next Period**
- 2-3 specific, measurable goals
- Tie them to business impact, not just activity

### Step 5: Practice Your Talking Points (10 minutes)

For each theme, have a 60-second verbal summary ready. Your written review is the artifact. The conversation is where impressions are formed.

## What Your Manager Wants to See

Let me save you some guesswork. Here's what most CS leaders care about:

1. **Revenue impact** — Did you retain and grow your book of business?
2. **Customer outcomes** — Are your customers successful? Can you prove it?
3. **Process contribution** — Did you make the team better, not just yourself?
4. **Strategic thinking** — Do you understand the business, or just execute tasks?
5. **Cross-functional influence** — Can you work effectively across teams?
6. **Coachability** — Do you take feedback and act on it?

Your self-assessment should directly address at least 4 of these 6.

## Ongoing Habits That Make Reviews Easy

### The Friday 5-Minute Brag

Every Friday before you log off, spend 5 minutes answering:
- What did I accomplish this week?
- Did anything go particularly well?
- Is there a number I can attach to it?

Log it in your Brag Book. Takes 5 minutes. Saves you hours at review time.

### The Monthly Metrics Check

On the first Monday of each month, update:
- Account health scores
- Retention/churn numbers
- Any notable wins or losses
- Progress toward quarterly goals

### The Pre-Review Data Pull

30 days before your review, start the prep. Don't wait until the week of. Pull your data, identify gaps in documentation, and fill them while the memories are still semi-fresh.

## Handling Difficult Review Conversations

### If Your Numbers Aren't Great

Don't hide from it. Address it head-on:
- "My retention rate this quarter was 88%, below our 92% target. Two of the three churns were driven by [specific external factors]. Here's what I did to try to prevent them, and here's what I've changed for next quarter."

Owning the narrative is infinitely better than hoping nobody notices.

### If You Want a Promotion

Make the case with evidence, not just tenure:
- Show you're already performing at the next level
- Point to specific examples of scope expansion
- Demonstrate strategic impact beyond your core accounts
- Have supporting feedback from cross-functional partners

### If You Disagree with Feedback

Pick your battles. But if you genuinely disagree:
- Acknowledge the perspective: "I see why it might look that way"
- Provide additional context they may not have
- Focus on what you'll do going forward
- Don't get defensive. It never helps, even when you're right.

### Peer Feedback Request Template

When you need to gather supporting feedback:

```
Hey [Name],

I'm putting together my self-assessment for this review cycle and I'd
really appreciate your perspective on our work together.

If you have a few minutes, could you share:
1. A specific example of how we collaborated well
2. Something I could do better in how we work together
3. Any impact you've seen from my work with [Account/Project]

Totally understand if you're slammed — even a few sentences would be super helpful.

Thanks!
```
